How Bar-Ilan University Manages 1,000 Students with monday CRM

Universities don't usually show up in CRM conversations. But Bar-Ilan University's continuing education division faces the same challenge any growing B2C operation does: hundreds of inquiries per week, multiple program tracks, payment plans, enrollment deadlines, and a small team expected to keep all of it from falling apart. monday CRM turned out to be a surprisingly good fit — and the way they structured it is worth stealing.

---

The Problem

Bar-Ilan's Division of Continuing Education runs dozens of programs simultaneously — professional certificates, academic enrichment courses, corporate training. At peak enrollment periods, the team was handling over 1,000 active student records at different stages: inquiry, application, payment, enrollment, and ongoing communication.

The old system was a patchwork of spreadsheets, email threads, and a registration platform that didn't talk to anything else. Advisors had no visibility into where a student stood. A student who paid their deposit might not get their welcome materials for two weeks because the information lived in three different places and nobody owned the handoff.

Operationally, the cost was real: dropped leads, duplicate outreach, and advisors spending hours each week just figuring out what needed to happen next.

---

The Solution

They moved student lifecycle management into monday CRM and treated each student record as a deal moving through a pipeline. The pipeline stages mapped directly to the enrollment journey: New Inquiry → Qualified → Application Sent → Deposit Paid → Enrolled → Active Student.

Each item held the full context — program interest, contact history, payment status, assigned advisor, and key dates. Automations handled the routine handoffs: when a deposit was marked received, the status updated automatically and a task was created for the enrollment coordinator to send onboarding materials.

The CRM didn't replace their registration system — it sat on top of it as the operational layer where humans actually worked.

---

Step by Step

Here's how they structured it, which you can replicate for any high-volume student or client operation:

1. Define your pipeline stages precisely. Vague stages like "In Progress" are useless. Map the exact actions that move someone from one stage to the next and make those the criteria.

2. Build a custom column set per stage. Early stages need contact info and program interest. Later stages need payment confirmation, start dates, and assigned materials. Use conditional visibility or mirror columns to keep items clean.

3. Automate the transitions. Every manual status change is a potential failure point. If payment confirmed = deposit paid stage, automate it. Connect to your payment processor or CRM forms via monday integrations or Make.

4. Assign ownership clearly. One advisor per student record. No ambiguity. Use the People column and filter each advisor's board view to their own items.

5. Use dashboards for volume management. At 1,000 students, you can't manage by scrolling. Build a dashboard that shows pipeline distribution, upcoming deadlines, and items that haven't been touched in more than five days.

---

Real Example

At the start of a new semester intake, the team runs a campaign to past inquiries who didn't enroll. In the old world, this meant exporting a list, filtering manually, and drafting individual emails.

Now: a saved filter surfaces every contact with status "Qualified" or "Application Sent" from the previous term. An advisor reviews the list, updates context notes, and triggers a follow-up sequence directly from the CRM. When a student responds and re-engages, the item moves forward in the pipeline automatically.

More importantly, when an enrolled student needs an official confirmation letter or payment receipt for their employer, the advisor used to draft it manually from scratch. That's where document generation came in — more on that below.

---

How to Get Started

If you're managing any kind of enrollment, onboarding, or client intake at volume, the structure above is directly portable. Start with your pipeline stages — get those right first. Then layer in automations, then dashboards.
